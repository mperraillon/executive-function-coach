
Constraints at work — a personal working reference

Guiding principle. Freedom is the enemy of focus and originality; the right constraint is the engine of both. Build limits deliberately rather than waiting for circumstance to impose them.

Workflow and productivity

•	Your bottleneck is your own synthesis and writing time — not the volume of inputs. Stop optimizing for more drafts, data, or reading, and direct your effort at protecting and widening the hours you spend editing and writing.

•	Run a “pull” system. Have students and co-authors submit the next piece only when you have cleared your review queue and can move it forward, rather than whenever they finish. Finish one thing before opening the next: stop starting and start finishing.

•	Protect attention with commitment devices. Block fixed writing time, set hard deadlines and start dates, avoid email first thing, keep daily lists short, and keep your phone out of sight. Task-switching leaves residue that degrades everything you touch.
Writing — the book, papers, and grants

•	Think slow, then act fast. Spend disproportionate time up front defining scope, deciding what not to cover, and testing cheaply — outlines and sample chapters read by non-experts — before committing to a full draft.

•	Use paired constraints on yourself. Preclude the familiar academic habits (jargon, hedging, exhaustive literature review) and promote specific replacements: one core idea per chapter, plain language, a strict word budget, and a single example per concept.
Teaching

•	Design for the most constrained student. Minimize working-memory load, anchor each new idea in a familiar one, move complexity onto slides and handouts, and write for the real, distracted student rather than an ideal one. What helps the least-prepared student improves the experience for everyone.

Working with people and ideas

•	Throw a “rope” with novel work. Anchor disruptive findings in established frames so they can be understood and adopted, and avoid getting so far ahead of the field that the work is simply ignored.
•	Set clear, impersonal rules. Explicit norms on authorship, credit, and expectations build trust and protect junior and more vulnerable collaborators from having their contributions taken.
